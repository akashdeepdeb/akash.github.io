Courses at UIUC

AP and proficiency credit

I started my freshman year with 19 credits. Looking back, I did well to take the 3 AP Exams - Calc BC, Physics C: E&M, Chemistry, and the PHYS 211 Proficiency test. Since I wanted to be a CompE, this allowed me to get out of the following courses: MATH 221, 231, PHYS 211, 212, CHEM 102, 103, 104; so, I didn’t have to take AP Computer Science A since CS 125 wasn’t required for my major.

  • AP Calculus BC

    Course Review
    When I was in high-school, I didn't actually take this course since I was in a different curriculum. However, I self-studied the course from Coursera and the Barron's books. If you're reading this and are in high-school, I highly recommend taking it, as getting a '5' is not very hard and it allows you to get placed out of the strict Calc I & II requirements and jump directly to Calc III (Calc I, II can easily be self-studied, so you don't have to spend 1 or 2 semester(s) with them.)

    References
    Coursera: Calculus I by OSU (course)
    Coursera: Calculus II by OSU (course)
    OSU: MOOCulus I (pdf)
    OSU: MOOCulus II (pdf)
    Youtube: My video on how to get a 5 in the test (video)


  • AP Physics C: E&M

    Course Review
    As with Calc BC, I hadn't taken this course formally (just the test.) I mostly prepared for it from the Barron's book and took time off from school to get help from professors. Other than that I watched MIT OCW's lectures online and Rice University's online edX course - both of which were extremely helpful. Doing 5-7 practice tests (basically, till your concepts are clear) is probably the best way to get a 5 in the test.

    References
    MIT: Walter Lewin's 8.02 videos (video)
    edX: Rice University: E&M - part 1 (course)
    edX: Rice University: E&M - part 2 (course)


  • PHYS 211 Proficiency Test

    Course Review
    In my Junior year of high-school, I had formally taken MIT's 8.01 Classical Mechanics on edX, taught by the great physics professor, Walter Lewin. It is perhaps the best course I have ever taken and 8.01's course quality is still unparalleled. Anyways, with 2 days left to prepare, I re-viewed all 36 hours of Classical Mechanics videos on YouTube and somehow passed the proficiency test. A stroke of luck you say? Maybe. Maybe not. When I took the test, I knew I had given many right answers because I got those answers mostly on first go. All in all, the test wasn't very hard. So, to prepare for it, you can watch Walter Lewin's lecture videos on YouTube.

    References
    MIT: Physics 8.01 - Classical Mechanics (video)
    UIUC: PHYS 211 UIUC (course)
    exscape: Notes by edX user exscape - Thomas Backman (pdf)


Fall 2015

These and some additional courses amounted to a 16 credit hours first semester. Honestly, this semester was a pain in the neck. I can think of a few reasons: Firstly, this was my first semester in US, this took some time to get adjusted to. I was stumped by the amount of homework we had to submit online and how strict the university was with homework deadlines. I recall missing many homework deadlines for PHYS 214 - something I corrected while taking PHYS 213 (which understandably resulted in a better grade in the course.) Secondly, I hadn’t done any past papers - back in high school this wasn’t very important. Although, in University, one of the best ways to prepare for any exam is to solve past exams.

  • ECE 110: Intro. to Electronics (w/ David Varodayan)

    Review
    ECE 110 is a good intro. to electrical engineering. However, I still think it is too easy - ECE 110 an 120 should be taken together, if possible, since, this allows one to take ECE 210, 220, 313 in the second semester if need be. The homeworks/exams are trivial if one has understood the concepts. If you are looking to get an A+ in this class, attend all classes, as each class has iClicker sessions. Also, do the past papers - during Fall 2015, many questions were the same.
